The Hokamas came from Madanbashi town (outskirts of Naha). It was said that post WWII, in those days, pretty much the whole village moved to Hawaii. At some point in time the Hokamas arrived on the Big Island, Papaaloa (Once pronounced = Papa A’loa , located on the Hamakua coast, within the Laupahoehoe Town.) The family was raised at the Kealani Camp which was also referred to as the “Hokama Camp” and sometimes referred to as “9 mile camp” (from Hilo). The plantation camps were seperated by ethnic origins -- Okinawan, Japanese, Chinese, Filipino , and Portugese.

Kamado was a “Luna” carpenter (Boss) on the flume (wooden chutes of water that transported the cane to the mill, which was also used to irrigate cane fields off season). Janet remembers that pipes were connected from the flume to our kitchen. The bedroom walls were all covered with newspaper, including Saturday colored comic pages with Little Orphan Annie, Dick Tracy, Blondie, and Superman. There was an outhouse where fruit wraps or newspaper were used as toilet paper by crumppling (roughen) it up to take the shine/smoothness out and to make it more absorbent. There was also “Furo” (bath-house) where potatoes were put on the wood fire as the bath water was heated. The floor was covered with goza. The house was one level, however it was built high so we could walk upright underneath. Laundary was hung under the house. From the house you could see the ships, and airplanes and whales too. Kameko remembered they had the biggest and newest house remembers moving to an empty house; Houses were owned by the plantation and when it became vacant anyone could move in.

In the back corner Uto made “Okolehau”(Moon Shine) during the depression era. Older children helped her make deliveries to other towns, but never on the plantations. Once, while using a donkey to haul “okolehau”, the donkey would not cross the river and Uto believed she saw a ghost. She would balance a ten gallon jar on her head to catch the bus to Hilo. She sat on the rear seat with the bottle secured next to her in a sack. In 1932 or 33 Uto was arrested and taken to jail . She persuaded them to put her husband in jail instead because she had so many children to take care. Jiro said this “craft” continued while living on Elm Street on Oahu too. According to others “Moon-shining” was not unusual.

A plantation Medical Team would go around to all the homes and refill their medicine kit. They left Ginsing, bandages, aspirins, and something like black tar. We did not have electricity so we used wood or kerosene stove, kerosene lamps, Ice Box, coal, or gas irons. Rice came in 100 lb bags and the empty bags were reused to fashion under garments. Anything they needed was bought at the store. They could charge it and pay for it with their next paycheck. At the store you could get almost everything you needed. The store also had a barber shop, and a public “furo”(bath).

Kids were expected to be out in the fields as soon as they could walk. They attended Kapehu Elementary, Ninole Elementary, John M. Ross School, and Laupahoehoe High school. Charlie played Baseball and Football. We were picked up by the plantation bus at 5 am . Everybody brought their own bento, like cod fish, sardine, squash, and rice. John M. Ross school did not have a cafeteria. Janet said, the roads would be lined with stones on both sides . When walking to school, we would hop on these, especially when the roads got muddy or flooding from the rain. We went to Japanese school right after English school.

There were many fruit trees around the plantation, near gulches and close to home, papaya, Vee Apple, mangos, guava, mountain apples, and wild strawberries. There was a pond near the cane field and the kids would go together to swim. Every family had their own vegetable plots. On Sundays the family worked on their own five acre garden. Jiro said, everybody went to work with a hoe, lunch can, and maybe a wooden umbrella or raincoat painted with linseed oil. There would be Lots of mosquites with denque disease too.
